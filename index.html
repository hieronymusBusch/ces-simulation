Economic interpretation

Baseline markdown. Each skill tier has its own observed wage markdown rH or rL. A markdown of r = 0.40 means women are paid 60% of the male wage: wF = 1 − r = 0.60. The implied taste/perceived-cost multiplier is φ = 1/(1−r), so the firm treats a woman paid 0.60 as if she cost 1.00 before the mandate. Under an equal-pay mandate, the paid wage becomes 1; if the discriminatory taste wedge remains, the firm’s perceived cost becomes φ = 1/(1−r).

Two independent switches. The high-skill switch forces wFH = wMH; the low-skill switch forces wFL = wML. If a switch is off, that tier stays at the discriminating baseline. If a switch is on, the woman's actual wage rises to the male wage, while the firm's perceived cost rises to 1+m in that tier.

Within-tier substitution. When a tier's switch is on, firms substitute away from women toward men inside that skill nest at the relevant elasticity σwH or σwL. This is why an equal-pay mandate can raise wages but reduce employment in the treated group.

Between-tier spillovers. If only one tier is treated, its composite unit cost rises relative to the other tier and the outer nest reallocates employment at rate σb. If both tiers are treated, the direction of the cross-tier effect depends on which composite unit cost rises more.

Key comparative statics: mH or mL ↑ → larger wage gain in the treated tier but stronger effective-cost shock. σwH or σwL ↑ → more aggressive substitution away from women in the treated tier. σb ↑ → stronger reallocation between high- and low-skill composite labor. Turning a switch off returns that tier to its baseline wage gap and removes the direct within-tier employment response.
Composite gaps: The top chart reports female/male − 1. The wage gap uses employment-weighted average wages by gender; the employment gap compares total female employment to total male employment across both skill tiers.
